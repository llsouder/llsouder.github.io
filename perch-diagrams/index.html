🎣 White Perch Water-Reading Guide

More realistic flow diagrams for bends, seams, eddies, marsh drains, and deep pockets — plus reference links to real hydrology/current-reading material

1. Outside Bend / Inside Bend

On a bend, the main push of water usually rides the outside bank. That scours depth and creates a better feeding lane. The inside bend tends to be softer, shallower, and more silted in.

Fishing translation: work the outer-bend edge, especially where the deeper trough softens into slightly slower nearby water.

2. Current Seam

A seam is where fast water meets slow water. Bait gets stalled or redirected there. Fish often hold just outside the heavier current so they can grab food without burning a lot of energy.

Fishing translation: present bait so it drifts along the seam line, not buried deep in the fastest flow.

3. Eddy Behind a Point

When the main flow slams into a point, grass edge, bulkhead, or other obstruction, it often leaves a softer spinning pocket behind it. That eddy can trap food and give fish a calm place to stage.

Fishing translation: hit the soft side behind the point and especially the line where the eddy reconnects to the main current.

4. Marsh Drain / Feeder Creek

On a falling tide, bait and shrimp get sucked out of marsh drains and feeder ditches into the main creek. Predators often set up right at the mouth, especially if there’s a little extra depth nearby.

Fishing translation: work the drain mouth and the first deeper water just outside it.

5. Deep Pocket / Scour Hole

Where flow accelerates or gets redirected, it can dig out a deeper pocket. Fish may rest there when the tide is strong or slide out of it to feed once bait gets moving nearby.

Fishing translation: fish the lip of the hole and the edge where the bottom rises back toward a flat or seam.

6. Flat Dropping into a Channel

As water drains off a flat, bait slides toward the nearest deeper edge. Fish often position right on that first drop because food naturally funnels there.

Fishing translation: fish the first clean breakline off the flat, especially if current is sweeping parallel to it.
